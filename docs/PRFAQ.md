## Press Release

### A product portfolio built by working backwards from both sides of hiring

**Hiring teams get a faster, higher-signal way to evaluate a product manager, and Mike Gebremeskel gets an honest way to stand out in a brutal market, through four real case studies.**

**Sachse, TX, June 2026.** Today Mike Gebremeskel launched a portfolio site that serves both sides of a hard problem in hiring. For the hiring team, it turns a low-signal resume into four in-depth case studies grounded in real artifacts and honest outcomes. For Mike, it is a way to be seen for the work rather than filtered out in seconds.

The market made the case for building it. Recent hiring data shows the average corporate role drew roughly 180 applicants per hire in 2024, with only about 3% of applicants reaching an interview ([CareerPlug, 2024](https://www.careerplug.com/recruiting-metrics-and-kpis/)). On the first pass, recruiters spend an average of 7.4 seconds scanning a resume ([Ladders eye-tracking study](https://www.hrdive.com/news/eye-tracking-study-shows-recruiters-look-at-resumes-for-7-seconds/541582/)). From the candidate's side, that is a brutally hard place to stand out. From the reviewer's side, it is a flood of near-identical resumes and very little signal about who can actually do the job.

Mike approached it the way he approaches any product: by working backwards from the users. There are two. The reviewer needs to evaluate many candidates quickly and reliably, and a resume rarely shows whether someone can frame a problem, make hard calls under uncertainty, and ship. The job seeker needs a way to prove that depth before a seven-second scan filters him out. A portfolio serves both, and the evidence supports it: decades of hiring research find that work samples, seeing what a person has actually built, are among the strongest predictors of on-the-job performance ([Schmidt & Hunter meta-analysis](https://www.plum.io/blog/schmidt-hunter-meta-analysis)). They are also rare in this field: fewer than one in six product managers have a portfolio at all ([Aakash Gupta](https://www.aakashg.com/product-manager-portfolio-examples/)), so a strong one stands out.

Each case study walks through a real problem, the decisions and tradeoffs behind it, and the measurable outcome, alongside a redacted artifact such as a Jobs-to-be-Done map, process maps from customer discovery, or shipped product screens. A recruiter gets the positioning in fifteen seconds. A hiring manager gets enough depth to decide on an interview.

"Being user-centric is not a line on my resume, it is how I work, so I built this by working backwards from both people in hiring," said Mike Gebremeskel. "The reviewer should know who I am in about fifteen seconds and be one click from real work. The job seeker, me, needs to be seen for that work before a seven-second scan filters me out. I set measurable targets for all of it, including a sub-1.5-second load and a 95-plus performance score, so the goals can be checked, not just claimed."

Using the site is simple. Land on the home page and take in the positioning and the four case studies. Open any one for the full story, problem to outcome, with artifacts you can view up close. Reach out through the contact form when you want to talk. No login, no friction, no hunting for an email.

The portfolio is live at the site address, with a contact form for hiring teams who want to start a conversation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### For recruiters and hiring managers

**Who is Mike, in one line?**
A product manager who enjoys the product development process and builds and runs fintech and B2B SaaS end to end. He cofounded Talisman, a venture-backed expense and accounting platform, and was the PM behind its award-winning 2024 launch. Before that he worked in pricing, account management, and master data at McKesson, a Fortune 10 company.

**What will I learn in the first thirty seconds?**
His positioning and the four case studies, each with a one-line hook. Enough to decide whether to keep reading.

**How do I know the work and the outcomes are real?**
Every case study points to a real artifact and names outcomes that can be verified, including a #1 SaaS Product of the Week win on Product Hunt, a This Week in Fintech award, and $113K in ARR. There are no invented metrics. Where a number is approximate, it says so.

**The case studies reference a former company. How is confidentiality handled?**
Only redacted artifacts appear. Client and personal names are removed, and the studies describe proprietary work rather than reproduce it.

**I want to move forward. How do I reach Mike?**
Through the contact form on the site. His email is not exposed publicly.

**Is he a fit for my role?**
The four case studies map to four distinct PM competencies: product strategy, execution and data-driven iteration, zero-to-one in a new market, and operating discipline (prioritization, specs, quality).

### For the PM-minded reviewer (how the product was built)

**Who are the users, and what are their jobs?**
Two, and the site is built for both. The reviewer, a recruiter or hiring manager, has to evaluate a flood of candidates fast: the average role draws around 180 applicants per hire and gets a roughly seven-second first scan, so they need high-signal proof, not more bullet points. The job seeker, Mike, has to stand out and be seen for real work before that quick scan filters him out. The two have opposite vantage points but the same unmet need: a fast, trustworthy way to judge whether the work is real and good. The portfolio is the artifact that meets both. Designing for both sides, not just my own, is the user-centric part, and it is the same instinct the case studies show.

**What research shaped this, and where can I check it?**
As part of the job search, Mike studied what actually moves hiring decisions and built from the findings:
- The market is crowded: roughly 180 applicants per corporate hire in 2024, with about a 3% applicant-to-interview rate. Standing out is genuinely hard. ([CareerPlug, 2024 Recruiting Metrics](https://www.careerplug.com/recruiting-metrics-and-kpis/))
- First impressions are fast: recruiters spend an average of 7.4 seconds on an initial resume scan, so positioning has to land instantly. ([Ladders eye-tracking study, via HR Dive](https://www.hrdive.com/news/eye-tracking-study-shows-recruiters-look-at-resumes-for-7-seconds/541582/))
- Showing beats telling: work samples are among the strongest predictors of on-the-job performance in decades of selection research. A portfolio is a work sample. ([Schmidt & Hunter meta-analysis, summarized by Plum](https://www.plum.io/blog/schmidt-hunter-meta-analysis))
- Portfolios are rare in product: fewer than one in six PMs have one, so a strong one is a real edge. ([Aakash Gupta](https://www.aakashg.com/product-manager-portfolio-examples/))

**Why a site instead of a Notion link or a PDF?**
A site gives a memorable custom domain, full control over the experience, and a public repo that becomes a second work sample. It also lets the work show three things a resume and a generic link cannot: how I think as a PM, that I build fluently with AI tools, and that I have real design taste, both technical and visual. Building and designing the site myself is the proof of all three.

**What is in version one, and what is deliberately left out?**
In: four case studies, a home page, an about page, a contact form, and a responsive layout on a custom domain. Out: a blog, a CMS, accounts, analytics dashboards, and theming. Holding scope is part of the point.

**How will we know it worked?**
Concrete, checkable targets, not vibes. The home page reaches Largest Contentful Paint under 1.5 seconds and scores 95 or higher on Lighthouse performance and accessibility; a first-time visitor can state the positioning within fifteen seconds (validated with a five-second test); every case study is one click from the home page; and the contact form produces real conversations. Anyone can verify the speed and accessibility numbers by running PageSpeed Insights or Lighthouse against the live URL. The full target list is in the PRD.

**Why is the repository public, and why both a PR/FAQ and a PRD?**
The repo is itself a work sample. The PR/FAQ aligns on the customers and the why; the PRD specifies the build. Carrying both, in the right order, shows command of each tool rather than just naming them on a resume.

**Why build it with Claude Code?**
It is an intentional, AI-assisted workflow and a signal of the AI fluency that product roles increasingly screen for. It is documented openly in the README rather than hidden.

**What are the biggest risks, and how are they handled?**
First, the work-sample framing only helps if the site itself is excellent, so the live site must stand fully on its own and the repo is a depth layer, never an excuse for a weaker site. Second, confidentiality, handled through redaction. Third, a static host has no backend, so the contact form uses a hosted form service rather than a custom server.
